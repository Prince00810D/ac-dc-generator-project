1. Introduction

AC and DC generators convert mechanical energy into electrical energy using electromagnetic induction.


AC Generators

  • Operates on Faraday's law of electromagnetic induction.
  • Uses slip rings.
  • Produces alternating current.
  • Used in industries and power distribution.

DC Generators

  • Uses split ring commutator.
  • Produces direct current.
  • Used in battery charging and portable devices.

2. Aim and Objectives

To design and construct a generator system that converts AC into DC using rectifier circuits.


  • Understand AC generator working
  • Convert AC output into DC
  • Study coil, magnet, diode and capacitor
  • Generate electrical energy mechanically
  • Observe difference between AC and DC
  • Improve practical knowledge

3. Construction

The construction involves semiconductor switches, PCB board, transformer, capacitor and magnetic components.

  • Input stage uses 12V battery
  • Switching stage uses MOSFETs
  • Transformer increases voltage
  • Control stage manages pulse signals

4. Working

Mechanical energy rotates the conductor in magnetic field producing AC current. A commutator converts AC into DC output.


  • Primary induction creates pulsating current
  • Transformer increases voltage
  • Slip rings direct current outward

5. Flowchart & Methodology

Mechanical Energy → AC Generation → Rectifier → DC Output

Faraday’s law induces alternating current in rotating coils. Split-ring commutator converts current into unidirectional DC.

Advantages

  • Efficient power transmission
  • Lower maintenance
  • Cost effective
  • Higher power output
  • Easy conversion to DC

Disadvantages

  • High maintenance brushes
  • Power losses
  • Voltage drops
  • Lower efficiency
  • Complex regulation

Applications

  • Battery Charging
  • DC Motors
  • Arc Lighting
  • Electroplating
  • Power Plants
  • Boosters

Conclusion

AC generators are efficient for large-scale power transmission while DC output is ideal for electronics, charging systems and industrial motors.
